For families

Honest answers to
the questions you're asking.

Most families come to us already worried, already tired, and unsure where to start. Here's some of what we wish more families knew.

Knowing when
Signs your parent may need a little help

Unopened mail, weight loss, the same dish in the fridge for days, falls or near-falls, missed medications, withdrawal from things they loved. None of these mean "nursing home" — most mean "a few hours of help each week."

Cost & coverage
What in-home care actually costs in Ontario

Companion care typically runs $30–$40/hr. Live-in care starts around $340/day; 24-hour active shift care runs $650–$900/day. Much of it is claimable on the Medical Expense Tax Credit, and most extended-health plans cover a meaningful portion. We'll walk you through what applies in your situation.

Hard conversations
How to talk to a parent who won't accept help

Lead with their independence, not your worry. Frame it as a companion (not a caregiver). Start small — a few hours a week. Let them meet the person first. We've done this with hundreds of families.

Comparing options
In-home care vs. retirement homes vs. LTC

In-home care lets your loved one stay in the place they know, with one-to-one attention, often at a fraction of the cost of a facility. We're glad to talk through the trade-offs honestly — even if our service isn't the right fit.

Common questions

The things families
most often ask us.

How quickly can care begin?
+
Most families have their free consultation within 24 hours, and care can begin within 3–5 days — sometimes the very next day for urgent situations. We never rush the matching process, but we don't make families wait either.
What if my loved one doesn't connect with their caregiver?
+
Just tell us. There's no awkward conversation required and no penalty. We'll quietly introduce someone new, taking everything we learned about your loved one's personality into account. The right match matters more than anything.
What kind of medical care do you provide?
+
We provide non-medical support only — companionship, daily-living help, medication reminders, mobility assistance, and personal care like bathing and dressing. Anything involving injections, wound care, or clinical assessments is handled by your loved one's nurses or physician. We work closely alongside them.
Are your caregivers really background-checked?
+
Yes — every caregiver goes through a vulnerable-sector police check, multiple reference calls, an in-person interview, and a paid trial shift before joining our team. We also carry liability and WSIB insurance on every visit. You'll see their credentials before they ever step into your home.
Do I have to sign a contract?
+
No long-term contracts. We use a simple service agreement that can be paused, adjusted, or ended any time with one week's notice. We earn your continued trust week to week — that's how it should be.
Is any of this covered by insurance or government programs?
+
Many extended health insurance plans cover personal support services, and some veterans' programs, LTCi policies, and long-term care benefits may apply. We'll walk you through what might be reimbursable during your free consultation and provide all the documentation you need.
What if my parent has dementia and becomes aggressive or confused?
+
Aggression or confusion during dementia is rarely about the person themselves — it's usually fear, pain, or overstimulation. Our dementia-trained caregivers know how to de-escalate calmly, redirect gently, and create predictable routines that prevent these moments from escalating. We never use restraint, and we'll always brief you on what happened and what we changed.
Can the caregiver give medications?
+
Our caregivers can remind your loved one to take medications and hand them their pre-organized blister pack or pill organizer. They can’t prescribe, measure, or administer injectables — those require a nurse. Many families use their pharmacist’s free blister-pack service to make this seamless; we’re happy to help arrange it.
Can you help with bathroom assistance and personal hygiene?
+
Yes — this is one of the most common needs we help with, and we know how vulnerable that conversation feels. Our personal-care caregivers are PSW-certified and specifically trained to preserve dignity during bathing, toileting, dressing, and grooming. We always match a caregiver your loved one feels comfortable with, and the first session usually involves your presence to make introductions feel natural.
What if I’m out of province and can’t visit often?
+
Roughly a third of our families have at least one adult child living far away. We do weekly written check-ins by email or video call, monthly care reviews, and can coordinate directly with your local family or your loved one’s doctor. If something feels off, we tell you immediately — not at the end of the month.
Do you do live-in care for couples?
+
Yes. We have caregivers experienced with caring for both partners in the home — whether one needs more help than the other, or both have similar needs. Rates for couples are typically 15–25% higher than single-person live-in care, depending on complexity.
What happens if there’s a fall or emergency?
+
Our caregivers are trained in immediate response — they assess for injury, call 911 if needed, and notify you and your designated emergency contact right away. They stay with your loved one through the entire response. Every caregiver carries our on-call coordinator’s direct number for guidance. We document every incident and share it with you in writing.
What about pets in the home?
+
Pets are often part of what keeps a senior feeling like themselves. We’ll match your loved one with a caregiver who’s comfortable around dogs, cats, birds, or whoever else lives there. Light pet care (refilling water, short walks, vet runs) can be part of the visit, and many of our caregivers genuinely love this part of the day.
What’s the difference between PSW and HCA?
+
A Personal Support Worker (PSW) is the Ontario-recognized credential for non-medical home care — about 8 months of accredited training in personal care, mobility, and dementia support. Health Care Aide (HCA) is similar training used in some other provinces. Both are qualified for the work we do at Hearthlane. We also occasionally welcome internationally-trained nurses whose Canadian licensing is in progress.

Coverage & reimbursement

Most families pay less than the sticker price.

Several programs help cover non-medical home care. We'll walk you through which apply during your free consultation, and provide documentation for every claim.

Medical Expense Tax Credit
In Canada, fees paid for an attendant or care for a dependant with an impairment qualify as a medical expense — claimable on your tax return. We provide annual receipts in CRA-ready format.
Extended health benefits
Many group plans (Sun Life, Manulife, Canada Life, Green Shield) include attendant or personal-support care as a covered benefit, typically up to $1,000–$10,000 per year.
Veterans Affairs Canada
VAC's Veterans Independence Program reimburses housekeeping, grounds maintenance, and personal-care services. We can bill VAC directly for eligible veterans.
Long-term care insurance
If your loved one holds an LTCi policy, in-home care is usually a covered service. We supply care logs, invoices, and any forms the insurer requests.
Ontario Home & Community Care
Government-funded care through your local Home and Community Care Support Services is available for some clients. We work alongside CCSS hours when they apply.
Private-pay flexibility
Pay weekly by e-transfer or credit card. No prepayment, no auto-renewal, no surprise charges. You only pay for hours actually worked.

Our story

A family-run care company,
built from a real family's need.

Hearthlane began at a kitchen table in Mississauga. Our mother needed help. We couldn't find a service we trusted with her. So we built one.

The founders

Three of us, one mom, and a year of figuring it out.

In late 2023, our mother fell while reaching for something in the kitchen. She didn’t break anything, but the doctor said she shouldn’t be alone all day anymore. The three of us — her adult children — sat down on a Sunday and tried to figure out what to do.

We called five care agencies that week. Two never called back. Two sent caregivers who felt rushed and transactional. One was warm and patient but quoted $8,000/month — for hours we knew our mom didn’t need. None of them sat with us long enough to learn who she was: that she likes the radio on in Spanish, that she takes pride in folding her own laundry, that she falls asleep in the recliner after lunch and shouldn’t be woken.

We hired a private caregiver through a neighbour’s recommendation. Maria — yes, the same Maria who’s on our team today — turned out to be wonderful. But the year of finding her was hard. We thought: if it was this hard for us, with the time and resources to look, what about everyone else?

Hearthlane is what we wished we’d had. A small, locally-rooted team that treats every family the way we’d want our own mom to be treated — and pays caregivers fairly enough that the warmest, most patient people actually stay.

Our promise

Every caregiver we send is someone we’d send to our own mother.

That standard doesn’t bend. We accept roughly 3 of every 20 caregivers who apply — not because the others are bad people, but because the bar for trusting someone in your loved one’s home should be high.

We pay caregivers fairly enough that they stay. We meet every family in person before care starts. And we answer our own phone — because the way we wanted to be treated when our mom needed help is the way we treat every family who finds us.

Our values

Three things that don’t change.

01
Dignity, always.

Your loved one is a person with a whole life — not a list of tasks. Our caregivers know their name, their stories, and their preferences before they start.

02
Honesty, even when it’s hard.

If we’re not the right fit for your family, we’ll tell you. If a care plan needs more or less, we’ll say so. We’d rather lose business than over-sell care.

03
Fair pay for fair work.

Caregivers are staff, not gig workers. They earn weekly pay, insurance, training, and backup support. That’s how the warmest, most patient people stay.
